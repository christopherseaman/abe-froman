

# The Gatekeeper Paradox: Credential-Construction Latency and Cognitive Load During Streaming-Service Password Generation

## Abstract

The proliferation of subscription-based streaming platforms has introduced a previously unexamined cognitive bottleneck at the point of account creation: the selection of a sufficiently secure yet memorable password. Despite decades of research into password hygiene (Florêncio & Herley, 2007) and decision fatigue in consumer contexts (Vohs et al., 2008), the specific psycholinguistic and executive-function demands imposed by streaming-service password generation remain conspicuously undertheorized. This paper asks: to what extent does the password-creation event in streaming onboarding induce measurable cognitive load, and does this load correlate with subsequent platform abandonment? Using a novel Protocol for Assessing Streaming-Service Password Stress (PASSPS), we administered a dual-task interference paradigm to 312 participants across six major platforms. Results indicate that mean cognitive load during password selection (λ = 4.72 on the modified Sweller-Kalyuga Index) significantly exceeded that of comparable digital registration tasks (p < .001, Cohen's d = 1.14), with a 23% elevation in galvanic skin response observed during the character-class requirement phase. Participants exposed to platforms requiring mixed-case alphanumeric passwords with special characters exhibited a 2.3× increase in what we term credential-construction latency. These findings carry implications for UX policy, platform retention strategy, and the emerging field of entertainment-access ergonomics.

## Introduction

The modern digital consumer inhabits a credential ecosystem of unprecedented density. What began as a modest access-control measure in mainframe computing environments — the single-use alphanumeric string — has metastasized, over the course of four decades, into a sprawling portfolio of passwords, PINs, passphrases, and biometric fallbacks distributed across an ever-expanding constellation of platforms (Bonneau, Herley, van Oorschot, & Stajano, 2012). The cognitive demands imposed by this portfolio have scaled nonlinearly with platform proliferation, a trajectory that Hargreaves and Lü (2019) characterized as the "subscription saturation hypothesis": beyond a critical threshold of concurrent service registrations, the marginal cognitive cost of each additional credential rises not arithmetically but according to a power-law function whose exponent (β ≈ 1.6) reflects compounding interference among stored access tokens. Nowhere is this escalation more acutely felt than in the streaming-entertainment sector, where a user seeking to watch a single episode of prestige television may first be required to construct a twelve-character mixed-case alphanumeric string incorporating at least one special character — a demand that, as will be demonstrated below, constitutes a nontrivial executive-function event.

Despite the maturity of cognitive load theory as applied to instructional design (Sweller, 1988), its extension to what might be termed *entertainment-access ergonomics* has been conspicuously neglected. The password-creation event occupies an unmodeled node in the user onboarding pipeline — a lacuna all the more striking given the well-documented relationship between extraneous cognitive load and task disengagement in adjacent consumer-technology domains (Sjöberg & Kapoor, 2016). The prevailing literature on password usability, while extensive, has treated credential generation as a context-invariant task, failing to account for the affective valence of the platform context in which the password is being constructed. A user generating a password for a tax-filing portal and a user generating a password for a service promising immediate access to the complete filmography of a beloved auteur are, in the extant models, treated as psychologically equivalent agents. They are not.

It is this asymmetry that motivates the present study's introduction of **credential-construction latency** (CCL) as a dependent variable distinct from general password-generation time. CCL incorporates not merely the temporal duration of the keystroke sequence but also the measurable physiological signatures of anticipatory reward conflict — the tension between the executive resources allocated to security-compliant character selection and the dopaminergic pull of imminent content consumption (Nakamura-Torres & Bjørnstad, 2023). From CCL we derive the **onboarding friction coefficient** (Ω), defined as the ratio of credential-construction latency to total registration time, which we hypothesize to be a significant and independent predictor of 30-day platform abandonment. Preliminary modeling by Delacroix, Tanaka, and Holm (2021) suggested that Ω values above a certain threshold produce irreversible disengagement, though their sample was restricted to Scandinavian early adopters and lacked physiological corroboration.

The present study asks, with precision: does the **character-class requirement phase** of streaming-service registration produce cognitive load distinguishable in both magnitude and kind from that observed in non-hedonic digital registration contexts? In this paper, we report findings from the first systematic application of the Protocol for Assessing Streaming-Service Password Stress (PASSPS) to a nationally representative sample of 312 participants, employing a dual-task interference paradigm, galvanic skin response measurement, and keystroke entropy analysis to quantify the cognitive burden of what has, until now, been dismissed as a mere administrative preliminary to entertainment.

## Methods

### Participants

Three hundred twelve adults (aged 21–58, *M* = 34.7, *SD* = 8.2; 53% female, 46% male, 1% declining to specify) were recruited via stratified convenience sampling from a metropolitan area with documented high streaming-platform density (≥ 4.2 concurrent subscriptions per household; see Lindqvist & Osei-Mensah, 2022). Stratification variables included streaming-service familiarity (assessed via the Platform Exposure Inventory; Chowdhury, 2018), self-reported password reuse tendency (dichotomized as habitual vs. situational), and binge-watching frequency (operationalized as ≥ 3 consecutive episodes in a single session, ≥ 2 times per week). Prospective participants were screened using the Credential Behavior Screening Inventory (CBSI), a 14-item self-report instrument with established internal consistency (α = .87) and test-retest reliability (*r* = .91 over a 3-week interval), validated in a companion psychometric study (Pham-Whitford & Okeke, 2024, *Journal of Digital Access Psychometrics*, 6, 112–129). Exclusion criteria included current or recent use of a password manager (within 90 days), professional experience in information security, and color vision deficiency (owing to the chromatic feedback elements of two platform interfaces). Twenty-three initially enrolled participants were excluded, yielding a final analytic sample of *N* = 312.

### Procedure and Dual-Task Paradigm

Participants attended a single 90-minute laboratory session during which they completed account registration on six major streaming platforms, presented in counterbalanced order via a Latin square design. Each platform condition corresponded to one of four password-complexity tiers constituting the **character-class requirement phase**: (a) lowercase-only, (b) alphanumeric, (c) mixed-case alphanumeric, and (d) mixed-case with special characters. Two platforms were assigned to the highest tier to permit within-tier reliability estimation, following the redundancy-counterbalancing protocol of Ekegren and Voss (1993).

Concurrent with each registration, participants performed a secondary auditory monitoring task — counting low-probability target tones (1000 Hz, 200 ms duration, *p* = .15) embedded in a stream of standard tones (500 Hz) — consistent with established dual-task procedures for cognitive load measurement (Paas & Van Merriënboer, 1994). The **modified Sweller-Kalyuga Index** (λ) was computed from secondary-task decrements using the formula:

> λ = (δ_baseline − δ_task) / δ_baseline × *k*

where δ represents tone-detection accuracy, and *k* is a scaling constant (*k* = 7.0) calibrated to Grünvald's (1978) original cognitive throughput norms.

### Physiological and Behavioral Measures

Galvanic skin response (GSR) was recorded continuously from the non-dominant hand at 256 Hz using Ag/AgCl electrodes, with artifact rejection applied at ± 3 *SD* from the participant-level mean. Pupillometric dilation (θ, in mm) was captured via a desk-mounted eye tracker (sampling rate: 120 Hz), synchronized to keystroke events. Keystroke dynamics — including inter-key interval, dwell time, and error-correction frequency — were logged by the PASSPS Keystroke Entropy Module (v2.1), a custom input-capture apparatus operating at the USB HID layer to avoid browser-level timing jitter (Marchetti & Sigurdsson, 2020).

### Statistical Approach

The primary analysis employed mixed-effects regression with platform nested within participant as a random effect, complexity tier as the fixed factor, and λ as the dependent variable. Pairwise comparisons were Bonferroni-corrected across tiers. The **onboarding friction coefficient** (Ω) was computed for each registration event as:

> Ω = CCL / *T*_reg

where CCL denotes credential-construction latency (in seconds) and *T*_reg denotes total registration time. Predictive models for 30-day platform abandonment used logistic regression with Ω, subscription cost, and content-library size as covariates.

All procedures were approved by the University Institutional Review Board (Protocol #2024-EAE-0047), which classified the study as involving minimal risk, a determination the authors consider generous given the observed distress levels during the special-character tier.

## Results

### Primary Cognitive Load Findings

The omnibus mixed-effects model revealed a significant main effect of registration context on cognitive load as indexed by the **modified Sweller-Kalyuga Index**, *F*(1, 310) = 87.34, *p* < .001, η²_p = .22. Mean λ during streaming-service password selection (*M* = 4.72, *SD* = 1.08) exceeded that observed during non-hedonic registration controls (*M* = 2.91, *SD* = 0.84) by a margin corresponding to Cohen's *d* = 1.14, 95% CI [0.97, 1.31]. This effect size surpasses the threshold for "large" by conventional standards and approaches what Bergström-Ivarsson and Kwok (2020) have termed "infrastructural salience" — the point at which a cognitive demand ceases to be incidental to a task and becomes, in the phenomenological sense, the task itself. ### Physiological Responses During the Character-Class Requirement Phase

Galvanic skin response data confirmed that the **character-class requirement phase** constituted the locus of maximal arousal within the registration sequence. Mean GSR elevation during this phase was 23.1% above the pre-registration baseline (95% CI [19.4%, 26.8%]), with a significant Phase × Complexity Tier interaction, *F*(3, 924) = 11.47, *p* < .001. Bonferroni-corrected pairwise comparisons indicated that the special-character tier produced significantly greater GSR than all other tiers (*p* < .001 in each case), while the contrast between lowercase-only and alphanumeric conditions did not reach significance (*p* = .34). Pupillometric dilation (θ) tracked GSR across the first three complexity tiers but exhibited pronounced ceiling effects at the special-character tier (*M*_θ = 4.88 mm, *SD* = 0.31), consistent with the pupillary saturation phenomenon documented in high-demand cognitive tasks (Beatty, 1982). ### Credential-Construction Latency and Symbolic Indecision

Participants in the mixed-case-with-special-characters condition exhibited a 2.3× increase in **credential-construction latency** (CCL) relative to the lowercase-only condition (*M* = 48.7 s vs. 21.2 s, *p* < .001). A temporal decomposition of the CCL signal, made possible by the PASSPS Keystroke Entropy Module's millisecond-resolution event logging, revealed that 41% of total latency in the highest complexity tier was attributable to what we designate *symbolic indecision epochs* (SIEs) — inter-keystroke pauses exceeding 3,000 ms occurring exclusively during special-character selection. The mean number of SIEs per registration event was 3.7 (*SD* = 1.4) in the special-character condition, compared with 0.4 (*SD* = 0.6) in the lowercase-only condition. Figure 2 illustrates the distribution of SIE durations, which was positively skewed (skewness = 1.82), suggesting that a subset of participants experienced episodes of what can only be described as prolonged symbolic paralysis.

### Onboarding Friction and Platform Abandonment

The **onboarding friction coefficient** (Ω) was significantly correlated with 30-day platform abandonment, *r* = .38, *p* < .01, after controlling for subscription cost and content-library size. Logistic regression indicated that each 0.10-unit increase in Ω was associated with a 14% increase in the odds of abandonment (OR = 1.14, 95% CI [1.06, 1.23]). These values exceeded the predictive benchmarks reported by Delacroix, Tanaka, and Holm (2021), who observed *r* = .22 in their Scandinavian subsample, suggesting that the present nationally representative cohort may be more sensitive to credential-induced friction than previously estimated.

### Exploratory Analysis: The Password Reuse Paradox

An unanticipated finding emerged among participants classified as habitual password reusers. Rather than exhibiting reduced cognitive load — as a naïve efficiency account would predict — reusers demonstrated significantly *elevated* λ values (*M* = 5.11, *SD* = 1.22) relative to novel-password generators (*M* = 4.43, *SD* = 0.94), *t*(198) = 4.12, *p* < .001. This pattern is consistent with classical proactive inhibition effects (Underwood, 1957) and suggests that the retrieval of previously stored credentials in a novel platform context generates mnemonic interference that inflates, rather than alleviates, the processing demands of the credential-construction event. The implications of this finding for password-reuse advisories are, it must be said, considerable, and are addressed in the Discussion.

## Discussion

The present findings suggest that the cognitive architecture of streaming-service password creation is not merely a scaled variant of generic credential generation but rather a qualitatively distinct processing event. As anticipated by cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988), the character-class requirement phase imposes extraneous load that competes directly with the germane processing resources the user would otherwise allocate to platform navigation and content selection. What distinguishes the streaming context, however, is the simultaneous activation of anticipatory hedonic circuits — the user is, in effect, cognitively proximate to leisure yet procedurally trapped in a security compliance task. This tension has been characterized elsewhere as the "gatekeeper paradox" (Nakamura-Torres & Bjørnstad, 2023, *Frontiers in Digital Leisure Science*, 11, 44–59), and our data provide the first empirical quantification of its magnitude within a controlled experimental paradigm.

The onboarding friction coefficient (Ω) emerged as a robust predictor of 30-day platform abandonment, independent of subscription cost, content-library breadth, and interface satisfaction ratings. Platforms for which Ω exceeded 0.35 exhibited abandonment rates approximately 1.7 times the baseline, a threshold we propose terming the "ergonomic Rubicon" of entertainment-access design. This finding is broadly consistent with the friction-sensitivity gradients reported by Delacroix and Yun (2021, *Journal of Subscription Economy Research*, 8, 112–130), though their work focused on payment-method selection rather than credential construction. It is worth noting that Moretti and Sandoval (2020, *International Review of Onboarding Sciences*, 3, 78–94) speculated that password-related attrition would prove negligible relative to pricing effects; the present data decisively contradict this conjecture.

Perhaps the most theoretically provocative result concerns the paradox of password reuse. Contrary to the intuitive expectation that recycling a familiar credential should reduce processing demands, participants who reported deploying passwords originally constructed for non-streaming contexts exhibited elevated cognitive load (λ = 5.11) relative to those who generated novel strings. This pattern is consistent with Tulving's (1972) encoding specificity principle and with the proactive inhibition literature more broadly (Underwood, 1957): previously consolidated credential schemas interfere with the retrieval and adaptation processes required for deployment in a novel platform context. Wicklund-Choi and Abramov (2022, *Cognitive Ergonomics Quarterly*, 19, 201–218) observed an analogous interference effect in PIN reuse across banking applications, lending cross-domain support to the generality of credential-transfer inhibition.

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the laboratory setting, while necessary for physiological measurement, may attenuate the ecological validity of the observed credential-construction latency values; in naturalistic environments, participants would contend with additional distractors including ambient television audio, household interruptions, and the presence of impatient co-viewers. Second, the sample was restricted to anglophone participants, and the psycholinguistic demands of special-character selection may vary substantially across orthographic systems — a concern presently being addressed through the cross-linguistic adaptation of PASSPS (Okonkwo & Fleischer, in preparation). Third, biometric authentication modalities (fingerprint, facial recognition) were not included as comparison conditions, a gap that limits the comprehensiveness of the present cognitive load mapping.

Future work should extend the PASSPS framework to longitudinal designs examining whether credential-construction latency at onboarding predicts long-term engagement patterns and content-consumption trajectories. Additionally, investigation of the neurophysiological substrates of the character-class requirement phase via functional near-infrared spectroscopy would permit finer-grained localization of the prefrontal demand imposed by special-character selection. Finally, the interaction between password stress and household-level subscription decision-making — particularly in multi-user accounts where one individual bears disproportionate credential-management burden — represents a fertile and largely uncharted domain within entertainment-access ergonomics.

Taken together, these results establish that the cognitive cost of securing access to entertainment content constitutes a measurable, predictive, and policy-relevant barrier to the equitable distribution of digital leisure resources across the subscribing population.

## Conclusion

The exclamation mark has long been understood as a marker of enthusiasm. It is a bitter irony of contemporary platform design that the context in which a user is most statistically likely to type one is not a message to a loved one, nor a celebration, but the mandatory special character in a password constructed under duress — a punctuation of compliance misread by the system as strength. Every streaming service that demands `!` in its password field should reckon with the possibility that what it has extracted from the user is not security, but a small, measurable piece of defeat.
