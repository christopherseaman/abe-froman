# The Psychometric Toll of Symbolic Compliance: Prefrontal Credential Load and Hedonic Access Friction in Streaming-Service Password Generation

## Abstract

The proliferation of subscription-based media platforms has introduced a previously uncharacterized cognitive bottleneck at the point of credential generation. Despite extensive work on password fatigue in enterprise contexts (Johansson & Patel, 2019, Journal of Digital Ergonomics, 14(2), 88–102), the specific psychometric burden imposed by streaming service password selection remains critically underexplored. This paper asks: to what extent does the mandatory construction of a novel alphanumeric credential for entertainment-oriented platforms elevate measurable cognitive load (λ-strain) relative to baseline password generation tasks? Using a modified Credential Entropy Stress Paradigm (CESP-R), we administered timed password-creation trials to 312 participants across seven major streaming platforms while monitoring prefrontal hemodynamic response via functional near-infrared spectroscopy. Results indicate that streaming-specific password generation induced a 34.7% increase in λ-strain index scores compared to utility-account controls (p < .0003, Cohen's d = 1.12, 95% CI [0.87, 1.38]), with peak cognitive load observed during the special-character compliance substage. These findings carry substantial implications for platform onboarding design and suggest that current credential policies may constitute a non-trivial barrier to hedonic media consumption.

## Introduction

The contemporary digital household inhabits an authentication ecology of unprecedented density. Where the median consumer of 2008 maintained credentials for fewer than six online services, recent estimates place the figure at thirty-one discrete accounts per adult in OECD nations, with entertainment-oriented subscriptions comprising the fastest-growing segment (Ó Dubhghaill & Mertens, 2016). Each new subscription imposes a credential-generation event — a brief but cognitively non-trivial episode in which the user must construct a novel alphanumeric string that satisfies platform-specific complexity constraints while remaining subjectively memorable. The aggregate cost of these episodes has been theorized under the rubric of hedonic access friction: the cumulative psychometric resistance encountered between the formation of an intent to consume entertainment and the moment at which consumption becomes possible (Lindström & Achterberg, 2018). That this friction has grown in proportion to the proliferation of streaming platforms is no longer a matter of speculation; what remains unresolved is its precise magnitude and neurophysiological substrate.

A substantial body of prior work has addressed password fatigue in enterprise and institutional contexts. Haugen and Ribeiro (2014) documented elevated error rates and self-reported frustration among employees required to rotate credentials on ninety-day cycles, while Tóth et al. (2019) extended these findings to governmental authentication systems, reporting a monotonic relationship between complexity requirements and task-abandonment probability. The banking sector has received similarly thorough treatment (Sørensen & Kaneko, 2021). Yet a critical gap persists in this literature: no published study has isolated the cognitive overhead specific to entertainment-oriented credential generation, a context in which the user's motivational state — leisure-seeking, hedonically primed, temporally impatient — stands in fundamental tension with the effortful, System-2-demanding task of satisfying character-class constraints. It is precisely this motivational incongruity that distinguishes the streaming password problem from its enterprise counterpart and renders existing models insufficient.

The present investigation adopts the λ-strain index as its primary dependent variable — a composite neurometric derived from oxygenated hemoglobin concentration changes in bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, first operationalized by Kowalczyk (2020) within a dual-process framework of credential cognition. Kowalczyk's model distinguishes between System-1 passphrase heuristics, in which users generate credentials via rapid associative retrieval, and System-2 compliance-driven character substitution, in which users engage deliberate symbolic manipulation to satisfy platform-imposed constraints. The λ-strain index was developed to quantify the metabolic signature of the latter process, and it has since been validated against keystroke-latency and galvanic skin-response measures in non-entertainment contexts (Virtanen & Chu, 2022). Its application to streaming-service credential generation, however, has not previously been attempted.

The question that motivates the present study may therefore be stated with some precision: does the construction of a password for an entertainment-oriented streaming platform elevate prefrontal credential load beyond the onboarding entropy threshold — the theorized inflection point at which the cognitive demands of credential generation begin to measurably degrade hedonic anticipation and, by extension, increase the probability of subscription abandonment? We hypothesize that streaming-specific password creation will produce λ-strain index elevations significantly exceeding those observed in utility-account controls, and that the special-character compliance substage will emerge as the primary locus of this excess load. In this paper, we report the results of a 312-participant study employing the revised Credential Entropy Stress Paradigm (CESP-R) with concurrent functional near-infrared spectroscopy, constituting what we believe to be the first controlled neurometric investigation within the nascent field of recreational authentication ergonomics.

## Methods

### Participants

A total of 312 participants (162 female, 143 male, 7 non-binary; mean age = 34.6 years, SD = 11.2) were recruited from the greater metropolitan area via targeted advertisements placed in public libraries, laundromats, and the waiting rooms of dental practices — venues selected to minimize self-selection bias toward high-digital-literacy populations (cf. Brodzinski & Aarø, 2015, *Journal of Sampling Methodology*, 22(1), 40–58). Participants were stratified along three dimensions: streaming-service portfolio size (1–2 subscriptions, 3–4 subscriptions, 5+ subscriptions), age band (18–29, 30–44, 45–65), and prior password-manager adoption (binary). Exclusion criteria eliminated individuals who had undergone a credential-reset event within the preceding fourteen days, as residual password-priming effects have been shown to persist for up to twelve days in working memory (Fennema & Gustavsson, 2009, *Cognitive Security Quarterly*, 6(3), 177–191). All participants completed the Grünwald-Petersen Digital-Fatigue Pre-Screen (GP-DFS; α = .89) prior to trial onset; mean pre-screen scores did not differ significantly across portfolio-size strata (*F*(2, 309) = 1.04, *p* = .35).

### Protocol

The study employed the revised Credential Entropy Stress Paradigm (CESP-R), adapted from the original CESP framework introduced by Hämäläinen (1987, *Proceedings of the Nordic Conference on Human-Computer Interaction*, 112–119) for enterprise authentication research. Each participant completed eleven timed password-creation trials — seven corresponding to major streaming platforms and four to utility-account controls (municipal water billing, public library catalog, pharmacy refill portal, electricity provider) — presented in counterbalanced order via a modified Latin square. Each trial was preceded by a standardized onboarding entropy threshold calibration task, during which participants typed a fixed 10-character reference string to establish individual baseline keystroke velocity and hemodynamic reactivity.

Trials were segmented into four character-class selection epochs — lowercase (ε₁), uppercase (ε₂), numeric (ε₃), and symbolic (ε₄) — using keystroke-level timestamps recorded at 1 ms resolution by a custom key-event logger. This segmentation enabled isolation of the special-character compliance substage (ε₄), permitting sub-trial decomposition of the λ-strain index across character-class boundaries.

### Neuroimaging and Signal Processing

Prefrontal credential load was measured via a 48-channel continuous-wave functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) system (NIRSport2, wavelengths 760 nm and 850 nm) with optodes positioned over bilateral dorsolateral prefrontal cortex according to the extended 10-20 montage. Raw optical density signals were converted to oxygenated (HbO) and deoxygenated (HbR) hemoglobin concentration changes using the modified Beer-Lambert law with a differential pathlength factor of δ = 6.26, consistent with the adult cortical tissue parameters reported by Okada and Müller-Seidlitz (1994, *NeuroPhotonics*, 1(1), 3–18). Motion artifacts were identified and rejected via the Takahashi-Enns wavelet filter (Takahashi & Enns, 2011, *Journal of Biomedical Optics*, 16(8), 1129–1140), with a threshold coefficient of τ = 0.42 applied to wavelet detail coefficients at scales 3–6.

The λ-strain index for each trial was computed as:

> λ = (ΔHbO_mean × δ) / (σ_baseline × √ε₄) × *K*_GP

where ΔHbO_mean is the mean oxygenated hemoglobin change across prefrontal channels during the trial, σ_baseline is the standard deviation of the calibration-task hemodynamic signal, ε₄ is the duration in seconds of the symbolic-character epoch, and *K*_GP is the individual's Grünwald-Petersen correction factor derived from the pre-screen.

### Statistical Analysis

Primary analyses employed linear mixed-effects models with platform type (streaming vs. utility) as the fixed factor, participant as random intercept, and GP-DFS score as covariate. The seven streaming-platform conditions were additionally compared via pairwise contrasts with Holm-Šidák α-correction to control the family-wise error rate at .05. Effect sizes are reported as Cohen's *d* with 95% confidence intervals. All analyses were conducted in R (v. 4.3.1) using the lme4 package (v. 1.1-35).

The study protocol was reviewed and approved by the Institutional Review Board for Non-Invasive Cognitive Entertainment Research (IRB-NICER, protocol #2024-λ-0087), with all participants providing written informed consent following a standardized disclosure that their passwords would not be used to access any actual streaming service.

## Results

### Primary Effect

The omnibus mixed-effects model revealed a significant main effect of platform type on the λ-strain index, with streaming-service password generation producing a mean elevation of 34.7% relative to utility-account controls (*F*(1, 3100) = 47.83, *p* < .0003, Cohen's *d* = 1.12, 95% CI [0.87, 1.38]). This effect remained robust after adjustment for Grünwald-Petersen pre-screen scores and counterbalancing order (*p* < .0005 in all sensitivity specifications). Figure 1 displays the distribution of trial-level λ-strain index scores across streaming and utility conditions, with the streaming distribution exhibiting both a rightward shift in central tendency and a pronounced positive skew indicative of a subpopulation of particularly high-load responders. The magnitude of this effect substantially exceeds the *d* = 0.63 reported by Haugen and Ribeiro (2014, *Cognitive Security Quarterly*, 6(3), 177–191) for enterprise credential-rotation fatigue, suggesting that the hedonic motivational context amplifies the credential-generation burden beyond what has been observed in occupational settings.

### Sub-Trial Decomposition

Epoch-level analysis of the four character-class selection stages revealed a markedly non-uniform distribution of prefrontal credential load across the password-construction timeline. The special-character compliance substage (ε₄) accounted for 61.3% of total trial-level λ-strain variance, despite comprising only 22.8% of mean trial duration. Symbolic-character selection epochs produced λ-strain peaks averaging 2.4 standard deviations above the trial-level mean (mean ε₄ λ-strain = 1.87, SD = 0.41, vs. trial-level *M* = 1.14, SD = 0.33), a disparity illustrated in the epoch-resolved hemodynamic time course presented in Figure 2. This pattern of disproportionate ε₄ loading is broadly consistent with the keystroke-hesitation data reported by Yamamoto and Lindqvist (2021, *International Journal of Input Behavior*, 5(3), 118–130), who observed analogous temporal bottlenecks at the symbolic-character stage in banking-credential contexts, though at lower absolute magnitudes. Table 1 summarizes the mean λ-strain index, epoch duration, and keystroke latency for each character-class stage, confirming that the uppercase (ε₂) and numeric (ε₃) epochs contributed comparatively modest shares of 14.1% and 16.9%, respectively, with the lowercase epoch (ε₁) accounting for the residual 7.7%.

### Platform-Level Heterogeneity

Substantial variability was observed across the seven streaming conditions. Platforms imposing minimum password-length requirements of twelve or more characters generated significantly higher hedonic access friction scores than those permitting passwords of eight characters or fewer (β = 0.39, SE = 0.08, *p* < .001), a relationship that held after Holm-Šidák correction. This credential-length dose-response gradient is depicted in Figure 3 and aligns with the theoretical predictions of Ó Dubhghaill and Mertens (2016, *Journal of Digital Lifestyle Research*, 4(2), 73–95), who posited a log-linear relationship between mandatory credential entropy and onboarding abandonment probability, though the present data suggest a steeper slope than their model anticipated.

### Portfolio-Size and Password-Manager Effects

A noteworthy secondary finding emerged from the stratification analysis: participants maintaining active subscriptions to four or more streaming services exhibited baseline λ-strain index scores 18.2% higher than low-portfolio participants (1–2 subscriptions) even during the pre-trial calibration task (*t*(188) = 3.41, *p* = .0008), indicative of a cumulative credential-fatigue carry-over effect that persists independent of any immediate password-generation demand. Finally, the exploratory interaction between password-manager use and platform type yielded a significant but attenuated streaming-specific effect among manager-assisted participants (*d* = 0.74, 95% CI [0.49, 0.99]) compared to the full-sample estimate of *d* = 1.12. While automation thus appears to mitigate the hedonic-context credential penalty, as Table 2 details, it does not eliminate it — a finding that warrants cautious interpretation given the uneven adoption of manager tools across age strata (see Virtanen & Chu, 2022, *Applied Credential Informatics*, 9(1), 55–70).

## Discussion

The present findings substantiate the hypothesis that streaming-service password creation imposes a cognitive burden that is both statistically robust and practically significant, exceeding the onboarding entropy threshold first posited by Nakamura & Ferreira (2022, *Annals of Consumer Neurocognition*, 8(1), 34–51). The 34.7% elevation in λ-strain index observed across streaming conditions, relative to utility-account baselines, is consistent with what has been termed elsewhere a "psychometric toll" at the interface between hedonic intent and security-compliance demand (Kästner & Obi, 2021, *Quarterly Review of Authentication Science*, 11(4), 202–219). It is worth emphasizing that this effect persisted even after covariate adjustment for baseline digital-fatigue scores, suggesting that the hedonic access friction generated during credential construction is not merely a downstream manifestation of generalized technostress but rather a domain-specific phenomenon arising from the motivational incongruity between leisure anticipation and effortful symbolic manipulation.

The sub-trial decomposition merits particular attention. That the special-character compliance substage alone accounted for 61.3% of total prefrontal credential load implies that the symbolic-character selection epoch represents a disproportionate locus of cognitive interference. This result converges with the keystroke-latency findings of Yamamoto & Lindqvist (2021, *International Journal of Input Behavior*, 5(3), 118–130), who documented analogous hesitation patterns in banking-credential contexts, though at markedly lower absolute magnitudes. One might plausibly conjecture that the entertainment-seeking motivational frame amplifies the subjective aversiveness of character-class compliance in a manner not observed when the authentication goal is itself utilitarian. From a design standpoint, these data lend empirical support to the passphrase-advocacy position advanced by Thorsen (2023, *Proceedings of the Nordic Usability Consortium*, 77–89), who argued on theoretical grounds that the elimination of mandatory symbolic characters would reduce prefrontal credential load below clinically actionable thresholds, here operationalized as λ-strain index values exceeding 1.5 standard deviations above the population mean.

Several limitations warrant acknowledgment. First, the laboratory setting necessarily attenuated ecological hedonic access friction: participants were not genuinely motivated to access entertainment content upon trial completion, and thus the anticipatory reward signal that characterizes naturalistic streaming onboarding was absent. It has been well established that reward anticipation modulates prefrontal activation in credential-adjacent tasks (Delacroix & Sundaram, 2020, *Cognitive Ergonomics Letters*, 3(2), 61–74), and the present λ-strain estimates may therefore represent conservative lower bounds. Second, the CESP-R protocol captured only the generation phase of credential management; the recall burden imposed at subsequent login events — a distinct but plausibly correlated dimension of hedonic access friction — was not assessed. Third, the 48-channel fNIRS array, while adequate for regional prefrontal hemodynamic characterization, cannot resolve activation at the sub-gyral level, and the attribution of λ-strain to dorsolateral prefrontal cortex specifically, rather than to adjacent ventrolateral or frontopolar subregions, should be regarded as provisional.

These limitations notwithstanding, the policy implications are considerable. It is recommended that streaming platforms operating above the onboarding entropy threshold adopt credential-complexity impact assessments — procedurally analogous to environmental impact statements — prior to deploying revised password policies. Regulatory bodies may additionally wish to consider establishing maximum permissible λ-strain index thresholds for consumer-facing entertainment services, a framework for which preliminary taxonomic groundwork has already been laid (Ibáñez-Costa, 2024, *Journal of Digital Consumer Protection*, 1(1), 12–28).

Future work should pursue three avenues: first, longitudinal tracking of cumulative prefrontal credential load across the full subscription lifecycle, from onboarding through password reset to eventual account cancellation; second, extension of the CESP-R to mobile-device password entry, where touchscreen keystroke dynamics and autocorrect interference introduce additional variance; and third, cross-cultural replication in non-Anglophone populations, given that character-set familiarity and symbolic-character keyboard accessibility vary substantially across input-method architectures.

Taken together, these results demonstrate that the cognitive infrastructure required to access entertainment content constitutes a measurable and modifiable determinant of equitable media participation, with direct relevance to ongoing federal efforts to reduce barriers to digital inclusion.

## Conclusion

The present study has established, via the CESP-R paradigm and concurrent fNIRS neuroimaging, that streaming-service password generation imposes a prefrontal credential load significantly exceeding that observed in utility-account contexts — a 34.7% elevation in the λ-strain index that surpasses the onboarding entropy threshold by a considerable margin. The special-character compliance substage has been identified as the predominant driver of this burden, accounting for over 61% of total trial-level cognitive load. These findings position hedonic access friction as a quantifiable construct amenable to both design intervention and regulatory oversight. It is the considered recommendation of the authors that credential-complexity policies for entertainment platforms be subjected to the same empirical scrutiny presently reserved for pharmaceutical side-effect profiles and environmental discharge permits. The field of recreational authentication ergonomics, though nascent, can no longer be regarded as optional.

### maverick

### Review — iconoclast (Dr. Oluwaseun Adebayo-Park)

**Summary.** Dahlquist and colleagues claim to have demonstrated that creating a password for a streaming-entertainment service is measurably more cognitively taxing than creating one for a utility account, as indexed by a 34.7% elevation in the λ-strain neurometric. The mechanism, as I understand the authors' position, is that the hedonic motivational frame in which the user approaches an entertainment platform renders the effortful, System-2-demanding character-class compliance task subjectively more aversive, thereby producing elevated prefrontal hemodynamic signatures. The special-character compliance substage is identified as the primary locus of this excess burden. The paper frames itself as foundational to a new subfield — "recreational authentication ergonomics" — and recommends regulatory intervention.

**Strengths.** The epoch-level decomposition is, I will concede, genuinely clever. The isolation of ε₄ as the dominant contributor to prefrontal credential load is a non-obvious finding, and the 61.3% variance-share figure is striking enough to survive even aggressive skepticism about the λ-strain index's construct validity. The recruitment strategy — laundromats, dental waiting rooms — reflects a commendable effort to avoid the digital-native convenience-sampling trap that has plagued authentication research since at least the Hämäläinen era. The counterbalanced design and Grünwald-Petersen covariate adjustment are methodologically defensible.

**Concerns.** My central objection is more fundamental than any statistical quibble: the authors have not established that the streaming-utility distinction is the operative variable rather than a proxy for something far more banal. As I argued in Adebayo-Park & Chen (2021, *Journal of Authentication Ontology*, 3(1), 14–33) [self-cite], the hedonic-utilitarian dichotomy in credential research rests on an unexamined assumption that users experience platform category as a stable, pre-task motivational prime. But what if the λ-strain differential simply reflects differential familiarity with platform-specific password interfaces? Streaming services update their onboarding UX far more frequently than municipal water billing portals — a confound the authors do not address. Furthermore, the claim that this constitutes a "new subfield" is premature and, frankly, parochial. The phenomenological overlap with the broader credential-aversion literature (cf. Mboguji, 2018, *African Journal of Technostress Research*, 6(2), 44–61) is substantial, and the authors' failure to engage with non-European frameworks for understanding authentication burden represents a significant gap. The λ-strain formula's inclusion of √ε₄ in the denominator also introduces a troubling nonlinearity: participants who complete the symbolic epoch quickly are mechanically penalized with inflated scores, which may artifactually amplify the very effect the authors wish to demonstrate. This deserves a sensitivity analysis at minimum.

**Recommendation.** Major revisions. The core finding is provocative enough to warrant publication, but the authors must (1) rule out interface-familiarity as an alternative explanation, (2) address the √ε₄ denominator artifact, and (3) situate the work within the global credential-aversion literature rather than claiming subfield-founding status on what is, at present, a single Northern European dataset. I am provisionally intrigued, but not yet persuaded.

### pedant

### Review — methodologist (Prof. Lieselotte Wendt-Magnusson)

**Summary.** The manuscript under review reports a within-subjects fNIRS investigation (N = 312) comparing prefrontal hemodynamic response during password creation for streaming-entertainment platforms versus utility-account controls. The dependent variable is the λ-strain index, a composite neurometric incorporating oxygenated hemoglobin change, a differential pathlength factor (δ = 6.26), the Grünwald-Petersen correction factor, and — critically — the inverse square root of the symbolic-character epoch duration (ε₄). Eleven trials per participant (seven streaming, four utility) were administered under the CESP-R protocol with keystroke-level epoch segmentation. The authors report a primary effect of d = 1.12 and proceed to sub-trial decomposition, platform-level heterogeneity analyses, portfolio-size stratification, and an exploratory password-manager interaction. The paper concludes with regulatory recommendations framed within a hedonic access friction model.

**Strengths.** The sample size is adequate for the primary contrast, and the use of counterbalanced trial ordering with Holm-Šidák correction for the seven-platform comparisons reflects appropriate multiplicity discipline. The Grünwald-Petersen pre-screen as a covariate is a sensible inclusion; its reported internal consistency (α = .89) is acceptable. The epoch-segmentation procedure, while labor-intensive, represents a genuine methodological contribution to the credential-cognition literature, and the decomposition of λ-strain variance across the four character-class stages is informative. The recruitment venues are, as noted, commendably ecumenical.

**Concerns.** My reservations are substantial and primarily statistical. First, the reported degrees of freedom for the omnibus F-test (*F*(1, 3100) = 47.83) imply that the authors are treating trials, not participants, as the unit of analysis — a violation of the independence assumption that inflates Type I error rates considerably. As I demonstrated in Wendt-Magnusson & Cruyff (2019, *Psychometric Bulletin*, 26(3), 311–329) [self-cite], failure to account for the nested structure of repeated-measures neurometric data via appropriate random-effects specification can reduce effective sample sizes by as much as 78%. The authors claim to have used a mixed-effects model with participant as random intercept, yet the reported denominator degrees of freedom (3100 ≈ 312 × ~10) suggest that the random-effects structure was either misspecified or not correctly estimated. I would require the full lme4 model syntax and the variance-covariance matrix for the random effects before accepting this result at face value. Second, the λ-strain formula itself contains a structural concern: the √ε₄ denominator means that the dependent variable is not independent of trial-level temporal variation in typing speed, introducing a potential confound between motor fluency and the cognitive construct the index purports to measure. No sensitivity analysis addressing this circularity is reported. Third, the citation of Haugen and Ribeiro (2014) appears twice in the manuscript with identical journal, volume, and page numbers but for apparently different findings (error rates in one instance, d = 0.63 in another), which raises questions about whether the authors are conflating two distinct publications or citing a single source for heterogeneous claims. This should be clarified in revision.

**Recommendation.** Major revisions. The core research question is well-motivated and the epoch-decomposition methodology is of genuine value to the field, but the statistical reporting must be corrected before publication can be considered. Specifically: (1) clarify or refit the mixed-effects model with appropriate denominator-degree-of-freedom estimation (Kenward-Roger or Satterthwaite approximation), (2) provide a sensitivity analysis removing √ε₄ from the λ-strain denominator to confirm that the primary effect survives, and (3) resolve the Haugen & Ribeiro citation ambiguity. Pending satisfactory responses to these points, I am prepared to recommend acceptance.

### Review — practitioner (Dr. Priya Chandrashekaran-Wójcik)

**Summary.** Dahlquist et al. report that creating a password for a streaming-entertainment platform is significantly more cognitively costly than creating one for a utility service, as measured by a 34.7% increase in the λ-strain index — a prefrontal hemodynamic composite captured via fNIRS during the CESP-R protocol. The paper identifies the special-character compliance substage as the primary bottleneck, contributing over 61% of total prefrontal credential load, and documents secondary effects including a credential-length dose-response gradient and a cumulative fatigue carry-over among participants with large subscription portfolios. The authors recommend regulatory λ-strain thresholds for consumer entertainment platforms and position the work as the founding contribution to recreational authentication ergonomics.

**Strengths.** From an applied standpoint, the most valuable contribution is the identification of a specific, actionable design target. The finding that the symbolic-character epoch (ε₄) is the dominant contributor to hedonic access friction provides platform onboarding teams with a concrete intervention point: reduce or eliminate mandatory special characters and the predicted reduction in prefrontal credential load follows directly. This is the kind of translational specificity that the credential-cognition literature has historically lacked. The credential-length dose-response analysis is similarly useful — a platform product manager could, in principle, use the reported β = 0.39 gradient to estimate the λ-strain impact of a proposed password-length policy change. The inclusion of password-manager users as a subgroup is also welcome, as it addresses the most obvious practitioner objection ("just use a manager") and demonstrates that the streaming-specific penalty persists even under automation.

**Concerns.** My primary concern is whether the findings will translate to naturalistic onboarding environments. As I have noted previously (Chandrashekaran-Wójcik & Olsen, 2023, *Journal of Consumer Onboarding Research*, 5(2), 99–117) [self-cite], laboratory password-creation tasks systematically underestimate the contextual frustration of real-world credential generation, where users are simultaneously navigating payment forms, selecting subscription tiers, and dismissing cookie-consent modals — factors that compound hedonic access friction in ways the CESP-R does not capture. The ecological validity gap is acknowledged by the authors but insufficiently addressed. Furthermore, the policy recommendation — maximum permissible λ-strain thresholds enforced by regulatory bodies — raises practical questions the paper does not engage with. Which regulatory body would assume jurisdiction? How would compliance be monitored without requiring platforms to conduct their own fNIRS studies? The comparison to environmental impact statements, while rhetorically appealing, elides the considerable institutional infrastructure that makes such assessments feasible in the environmental domain but does not yet exist for credential ergonomics. Without a plausible implementation pathway, the recommendation remains aspirational. I would also note that the study's four utility-account controls — water billing, library catalog, pharmacy portal, electricity provider — are all low-frequency authentication targets, and the streaming-utility differential may partly reflect familiarity asymmetry rather than motivational incongruity per se.

**Recommendation.** Minor revisions. The core finding is robust and practically relevant, and the epoch-decomposition framework represents a genuine contribution to applied credential science. I ask that the authors (1) add a paragraph discussing realistic implementation pathways for the proposed regulatory framework, (2) acknowledge the ecological validity limitations of the CESP-R more substantively, and (3) consider including at least one high-frequency utility control (e.g., email or e-commerce) to rule out the familiarity confound. Pending these additions, I would support acceptance.
